Module 01 — Scene of the Crime

What actually happened?

Before you analyse anything, you need the facts. Read the case outline carefully — the details matter, and there are traps ahead that depend on you reading closely.

First — what is Peloton?

🚴 The Company

Peloton is an American fitness technology company founded in 2012. They sell premium connected exercise bikes and treadmills ($1,500–$2,500+) with built-in screens that stream live and on-demand instructor-led workout classes. Think of it as a spin class you take at home, taught by a real instructor, with real music — all subscribed monthly. At its peak, Peloton had over 5 million members and was valued at $50 billion.

🎵 Why Music Matters to Peloton

Peloton's instructors build classes around specific songs — the music is the experience, not background noise. Peloton's own marketing stated consumers rank music as "one of the top aspects of the brand." Instructors chose tracks spontaneously, in real time, to match workout intensity. This made the product feel alive — and made the licensing problem nearly impossible to solve operationally.

Sync Licensing Copyright Infringement NMPA · 14 Publishers S.D.N.Y. 2019–2020

🚴 Peloton

Peloton sold premium connected fitness bikes at $2,000+. Instructors curated live playlists featuring Rihanna, Justin Timberlake, Bruno Mars, Lady Gaga, Katy Perry. Their own marketing stated: "consumers rank music as one of the top aspects of the brand." They had obtained some sync licenses — but left thousands of songs uncleared.

🎵 The Publishers (NMPA)

Fourteen music publishers, coordinated through the NMPA, sued in March 2019 for $150M. After discovery revealed deeper infringement, the claim expanded to 2,468 songs and $300M. Statutory damages: up to $150,000 per willfully infringed work — no need to prove actual losses.

"Unlike performance licenses — available through blanket deals with ASCAP, BMI, or SESAC — sync rights are controlled by individual publishers with no centralised clearinghouse. Each publisher sets its own terms and can refuse to license. Only 2% of songs in the lawsuit were owned by a single publisher." — Core structural issue in the case

⚖️ Peloton Countersues — The Sherman Act Gambit

Peloton filed antitrust counterclaims under the Sherman Act (1890), alleging a "hub-and-spoke conspiracy": that the NMPA coordinated publishers to collectively refuse individual negotiations and force Peloton into a single high-priced deal. Judge Denise Cote dismissed all counterclaims. The Noerr-Pennington doctrine protected pre-litigation coordination; Peloton also failed to define a proper relevant market.

⚡ Question 1 of 10 — Read carefully
Peloton had obtained sync licenses for some songs before the lawsuit. What does this tell us about their liability for the remaining unlicensed songs?
A It significantly reduces their liability — partial compliance shows good faith effort.
B It potentially increases liability — having some licenses proves they understood the requirement, making unlicensed use more likely to be deemed wilful.
C It has no bearing — copyright liability is the same regardless of intent or knowledge.
D It means they are only liable for the difference in licensing fees, not statutory damages.
The trap: Many students assume partial compliance = reduced exposure. The opposite is true. US copyright law awards enhanced statutory damages for wilful infringement — up to $150,000 per work vs. $750 minimum. By obtaining some sync licenses, Peloton demonstrated they knew sync clearance was required. Using thousands of other songs without clearance is therefore wilful by definition, maximising their exposure.
⚡ Question 2 of 10
Peloton's Sherman Act counterclaim alleged a "hub-and-spoke conspiracy." How should we assess this strategic move?
A A smart and bold legal manoeuvre — it forced publishers to justify their collective negotiating structure.
B Legally valid but ultimately settled before a ruling could confirm it.
C A costly strategic mistake — it failed on two independent legal grounds, extended litigation, added reputational damage, and distracted from a faster settlement.
D Irrelevant to the outcome — antitrust claims never affect copyright disputes.
The trap: The bold legal move looks clever on paper. In reality, Judge Cote dismissed it entirely on two separate grounds: (1) Noerr-Pennington doctrine shields pre-litigation coordination; (2) Peloton couldn't define its "relevant market" correctly. The counterclaim prolonged proceedings, generated more negative press, and consumed legal fees — all while the core infringement liability was never in serious doubt. Bold ≠ smart when your core position is losing.

Module 02 — Case Timeline

The sequence of events

A licensing crisis rarely arrives overnight. Trace how this unfolded from the first lawsuit to the post-settlement era.

March 2019
Lawsuit Filed $150M
Fourteen music publishers, coordinated through the NMPA, file suit in the Southern District of New York. They allege Peloton used approximately 1,000 copyrighted songs in streamed fitness classes without synchronisation licenses. Case: Downtown Music Publishing v. Peloton Interactive, 1:19-cv-02426.
September 2019
Claim Expands $300M
Discovery reveals the infringement was far deeper than originally alleged. The claim expands to 2,468 songs and $300M in damages. Statutory damages at $150,000 per wilfully infringed work create maximum theoretical exposure of over $370M. Peloton files its Sherman Act antitrust counterclaims.
Late 2019
Counterclaims Dismissed
Judge Denise Cote dismisses all of Peloton's antitrust counterclaims. The Noerr-Pennington doctrine protects the publishers' pre-litigation coordination. Peloton's market definition also fails scrutiny. Peloton's only legal counterattack is gone.
February 2020
Settlement Confidential terms
The parties settle on confidential terms — no public dollar figure. However, Peloton's earnings report that quarter reveals $49.3M in litigation and settlement costs. A joint collaboration agreement for future licensing is also established.
Post-Settlement
Artist Series Era Transformation
With proper sync licenses now in place, Peloton launches premium "Artist Series" classes featuring Beyoncé, The Beatles, Ed Sheeran, and others. The lawsuit that almost destroyed its music programme becomes the foundation of its most premium content offering — and a price discrimination strategy.
⚡ Question 3 of 10
The settlement amount was never publicly confirmed, but Peloton reported $49.3M in "litigation and settlement costs." Which of the following is the most analytically accurate statement about this figure?
A $49.3M proves the publishers accepted a low settlement far below their $300M claim, suggesting the claim was inflated from the start.
B $49.3M likely includes legal fees and settlement payment combined — the actual settlement payout is unknown, and $49.3M may still fall within the ZOPA's lower range plus costs.
C $49.3M is exactly what Peloton paid the publishers — earnings reports always separate legal fees.
D The figure is irrelevant since the settlement was confidential.
Precision matters in case analysis. Earnings reports typically aggregate litigation-related expenses — legal fees, settlement payments, related costs — under a single line. Separating what went to publishers vs. lawyers is impossible from public data alone. The $49.3M figure is a floor indicator and a signalling device, not a confirmed settlement number. Analysts who treat it as a confirmed payment are reading more certainty into the data than it contains.

Module 03 — The Licensing Maze

Why was this so complicated?

Understanding why Peloton couldn't just "buy a music license" requires understanding how music rights are actually structured. This is the architecture that made the crisis possible.

🎤

Performance License

Covers playing music publicly — radio, venues, streaming audio. Available through blanket deals with ASCAP, BMI, or SESAC. One payment covers millions of songs. Peloton had these. But this was not enough.

🎬

Synchronisation (Sync) License

Required whenever music is paired with video — advertisements, films, fitness classes, any visual content. No centralised clearinghouse exists. Every publisher negotiates individually. Each can say no. There is no blanket sync market.

⚙️

Fractional Rights Complexity

Only 2% of songs in the lawsuit had a single owner. Most songs had multiple co-writers represented by different publishers. Every fractional rights holder must separately grant clearance. One refusal blocks the whole song.

🎲

Spontaneous Instructor Selection

Peloton's instructors chose music in real-time during classes — creating a pre-clearance impossibility problem. You cannot clear songs that haven't been chosen yet. This operational model was fundamentally incompatible with sync licensing law.

"Is the complexity of music licensing necessary? Or is it a structural feature that benefits rights holders by creating barriers to entry and leverage asymmetry?" — Prof. Dr. Markus Rach, Lecture 10
⚡ Question 4 of 10 — Conceptual trap
Peloton had valid performance licenses through ASCAP/BMI/SESAC. Why were these insufficient to protect them from the NMPA lawsuit?
A Because ASCAP/BMI/SESAC don't actually cover popular artists — only independent musicians use these organisations.
B Because Peloton's performance licenses had expired before the lawsuit was filed.
C Because performance licenses cover the right to publicly play music, but sync licenses are a separate right — required specifically when music is paired with video. These are legally distinct, and ASCAP/BMI deals do not include sync rights.
D Because fitness classes are not considered "public" performances under US copyright law.
This is the foundational error Peloton made. Performance rights and synchronisation rights are legally distinct and separately held. A blanket ASCAP deal lets you play music at a venue or stream it as audio — it grants nothing when that music is embedded in a video recording. Peloton's classes were video content. Every song in every class required its own sync license, from its own publisher, negotiated individually. There is no shortcut.
⚡ Question 5 of 10
Peloton's instructors selected music spontaneously during live classes. What is the most accurate characterisation of this from a licensing perspective?
A Spontaneous selection is a legal grey area — it is closer to a live performance than a sync and should attract different rules.
B It is operationally inconvenient but has no specific legal implications beyond the general licensing obligation.
C It represents a fundamental structural incompatibility — pre-clearance is required by law, but spontaneous selection makes pre-clearance impossible, meaning the business model itself was built on an illegal foundation.
D Spontaneous selection removes wilfulness — you cannot be wilfully infringing if you didn't plan the song choice in advance.
The operational model was the problem. Sync law requires clearance before use — that is non-negotiable. Peloton's real-time instructor model was structurally incompatible with that requirement. The correct response was to either (a) build a cleared library and restrict instructors to it, or (b) obtain blanket sync deals in advance. Instead, Peloton did neither and allowed an illegal model to scale for years. On wilfulness: courts assess whether the infringer knew licensing was required — not whether they knew which song would be played. Having partial licenses confirms they knew.

⚠️ Peloton's Only Structural Argument

The court acknowledged that fitness music might be "interchangeable" — Peloton could theoretically operate with lesser-known or license-free tracks. This was Peloton's one argument for a stronger BATNA. But read the next question carefully before you accept this logic.

⚡ Question 6 of 10 — Classic trap
The court noted that fitness music is "interchangeable" — Peloton could use royalty-free or lesser-known tracks instead of popular songs. Does this mean Peloton had a strong BATNA?
A Yes — if music is interchangeable, publishers have no real leverage. Peloton could simply switch to royalty-free music and walk away.
B No — the court's "interchangeability" finding is a legal test, not a brand reality. Peloton's premium positioning at $2,000+ per bike demands popular, recognisable music. Switching to royalty-free tracks would destroy the brand proposition entirely.
C Partially — Peloton could have used royalty-free music for new classes while keeping popular music for its existing back catalogue.
D The question is irrelevant since the case was already in litigation — BATNA only matters before lawsuits are filed.
Brand is a BATNA constraint. The court's "interchangeability" statement addresses antitrust market definition — it is a legal test, not a business strategy assessment. In reality, Peloton's entire value proposition rested on a premium experience. Users complained loudly when popular songs were removed. A $2,000+ bike paired with stock workout music is not a Peloton — it is a generic exercise bike with a screen. Peloton's realistic BATNA was far weaker than the legal argument suggested. This is the same logic as saying "Hermès could sell canvas bags instead of leather."
⚡ Question 7 of 10 — Compare & contrast
Compare Peloton v. NMPA with the UMG v. TikTok standoff from the previous lecture. What is the most significant structural difference between the two power dynamics?
A In the TikTok case, both sides had roughly equal power. In the Peloton case, publishers were slightly stronger.
B The cases are fundamentally similar — both involve music publishers extracting value from a platform that underestimated them.
C In UMG v. TikTok, both sides understood the power balance and engaged in strategic negotiation. In Peloton v. NMPA, Peloton never understood its position — it built a business on an illegal model, ignored the imbalance, and was forced into litigation rather than negotiating proactively.
D The Peloton case was actually a stronger negotiating position for the platform because fitness content does not depend on specific songs the way TikTok depends on trending music.
Awareness of power is itself a form of power. TikTok understood the dynamic — they simply miscalculated UMG's willingness to walk. Peloton never understood the dynamic at all. They built an entire operating model that violated sync law, scaled it for years, and then tried to litigate their way out instead of negotiating their way in. The lesson: both cases confirm that understanding your power position before you act is the prerequisite for any strategic outcome. Peloton had no strategy — only a lawsuit to respond to.

Module 05 — BATNA & ZOPA Analysis

Where did the deal space sit?

Apply your negotiation frameworks to the actual numbers. Identify where each party's walk-away position sat, whether a ZOPA existed, and why the settlement fell where it did.

📌 Publishers' BATNA

Litigation itself. With $150,000 per song in statutory damages, clear evidence of unlicensed use, and catalogue covering Rihanna to Bruno Mars — the publishers had an exceptionally strong legal alternative to any deal. Their BATNA was essentially: go to trial and likely win big.

📌 Peloton's BATNA

Theoretically: switch to royalty-free music. Practically: destroy the brand, trigger user exodus, collapse the premium price point. Peloton's realistic BATNA was proactively negotiating individual publisher deals at market rate — which they had failed to do. Their actual BATNA at lawsuit stage was: settle quickly or face trial catastrophe.

⚡ Question 8 of 10 — Emotional trap
The publishers originally claimed $300M. The reported settlement costs were $49.3M. Does this prove the publishers were being unreasonably greedy with their initial claim?
A Yes — a gap of $250M between claim and settlement shows the claim was exaggerated and designed to intimidate rather than reflect genuine damages.
B Yes — any claim more than 5× the likely settlement is bad-faith litigation.
C No — the $300M figure reflects a legally valid calculation of statutory damages for wilful infringement at scale. Claiming maximum statutory damages is a standard and legitimate opening position. The settlement figure includes unknowns (legal fees, future licensing, collaboration value). The gap reflects negotiation dynamics, not dishonesty.
D No — publishers should always claim the maximum because courts will correct inflated claims anyway.
The emotional reaction to "greedy publishers" is a trap. Statutory damages exist precisely to deter wilful infringement at scale — Congress set the numbers deliberately high. With 2,468 songs and evidence of knowing infringement, $300M was the arithmetically correct maximum claim, not an invented number. The settlement reflects (a) litigation risk, (b) the value of a future licensing relationship, (c) confidential terms we don't fully know, and (d) legal fees bundled in reported costs. Dismissing the claim as "greedy" misunderstands how copyright law is designed to work.
⚡ Question 9 of 10
Post-settlement, Peloton launched premium "Artist Series" classes featuring Beyoncé, The Beatles, and Ed Sheeran — and used these to price discriminate. What does this reveal about the true cost-benefit logic of the settlement?
A It shows the settlement was too low — publishers should have demanded revenue sharing in perpetuity.
B The settlement transformed a liability into a strategic asset. Proper licensing didn't just remove legal risk — it enabled Peloton to build premium content that further justified its price premium, turning a court-enforced cost into a competitive moat.
C The Artist Series was coincidental — Peloton would have launched it regardless of the litigation.
D It proves Peloton was right not to settle earlier — the post-settlement relationship was worth more than any licensing deal would have been.
This is the strategic twist in the case. What began as pure legal liability became, post-settlement, a structural advantage. The Artist Series — enabled by proper sync licenses — allowed Peloton to create exclusive, branded workout experiences with globally recognised artists. This is price discrimination and brand premiumisation in action. The deeper lesson: complying with licensing law from day one would have given Peloton this same capability years earlier, at a fraction of the eventual cost.

⚡ Question 10 of 10 — Synthesis
What is the single most important strategic lesson from Peloton v. NMPA for a licensing professional?
A Always negotiate the lowest possible licensing fees — the cost savings justify the legal risk of minimal compliance.
B Antitrust law can be used offensively to neutralise rights holders who coordinate their licensing demands.

The deepest lesson in this case is not about copyright — it is about preparation. Peloton's mistake was not that they got sued. It was that they built and scaled an entire business model without understanding the legal architecture it depended on. Had they consulted a licensing attorney in 2012, they could have built a cleared music library, negotiated proactively, and launched Artist Series classes from year one. Every dollar of the $49.3M in reported costs, every reputational bruise, every moment of lost product development focus — all of it was the price of not doing the licensing homework upfront.

Case Conclusions — What the analysis tells us

vs. UMG / TikTok

UMG understood its leverage and deployed it strategically. Peloton never understood the legal landscape it operated in. Both cases are power stories — but one was strategic theatre; the other was a preventable catastrophe.

The $49.3M lesson

Proper sync licensing from day one would have cost a fraction of litigation. The "Artist Series" that Peloton eventually launched could have been their opening gambit. Legal compliance was never a cost — it was a delayed competitive asset.

  • Sync licenses and performance licenses are legally distinct — having one does not provide the other. Know the full rights architecture of your content model before you build.
  • Partial compliance with a legal requirement is not a defence. It may be treated as evidence that you knew the requirement existed, making remaining violations wilful.
  • Statutory damages exist to deter wilful infringement at scale — $150,000 per work is not an opening offer. It is the law.
  • A company's BATNA is constrained by its brand positioning. The court's legal finding of "interchangeable music" was irrelevant to Peloton's real strategic alternatives.
  • Leverage is built before the negotiation begins. Peloton arrived at the table with a lawsuit, no BATNA, and a counterclaim that failed. That is not a negotiating position — it is damage control.
  • Post-settlement, the relationship became collaborative and valuable. That value was always available — it just required doing the licensing work first.
